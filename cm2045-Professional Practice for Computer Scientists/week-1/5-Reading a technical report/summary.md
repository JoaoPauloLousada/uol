# Reading a Technical Report — Class Summary

## Overview

This lesson uses a PhD thesis as a practical example to teach how to read and structure technical/research documents efficiently.

---

## Key Topics

### 1. Structure of a Research Document

A well-structured research document typically contains:

- **Title & Author** — identification and institutional context
- **Abstract** — high-level summary of the research
- **Table of Contents** — navigation aid with numbered chapters
- **Table of Figures / Table of Tables** — quick access to visual data
- **Introduction** — sets the scene, often includes a literature review
- **Literature Review** — surveys existing research; substantiates claims
- **Methodology** — describes the research process step by step
- **Research Chapters** — independent studies or empirical work
- **Evaluation / Discussion** — outcomes, ramifications, connections
- **Conclusion** — summary findings, limitations, future directions

### 2. How to Read a Research Paper Efficiently

Recommended reading order to avoid wasting time:

1. **Abstract** — determine if the paper is relevant
2. **Conclusion / Evaluation** — understand outcomes and findings
3. **Introduction** — get the framing and scope
4. **Full paper** — only if it passes the above filters

> Skimming for keywords in the abstract allows quick relevance judgement before committing hours of reading.

### 3. The Abstract

- Summarises key elements and takeaways without detail
- Usually has no citations
- May include summary statistics or participant counts for context
- Primary tool for deciding whether to engage further with the work

### 4. Document Structure and Information Architecture

- Chapters, headings, and subheadings act like navigation on a website
- Depth of subheadings should match depth of discussion (e.g. 2 levels for broad topics, 3+ for granular empirical work)
- Each chapter should open with an introduction and close with a summary that bridges to the next chapter

### 5. The Literature Review

- Reviews prior research to frame the new work
- Establishes that the work is novel and not duplicating others
- Early work cites foundational pioneers; later sections move to contemporary research
- Can be a standalone chapter or distributed across early chapters depending on field conventions

### 6. The Methodology Chapter

- Describes the research process in replicable steps (like a recipe)
- Covers apparatus, participants, data collection, and analysis approaches
- Enables others to understand and reproduce the study
- Depth of subheadings increases as the methods become more granular

### 7. Figures and Tables

- Key tools for accessibility — compress complex ideas into visual form
- External researchers often navigate directly to figures/tables for key outcomes
- Good researchers produce many figures and tables to support their writing
- A single figure can describe structure or process more clearly than paragraphs of text

### 8. PhD Thesis Structure (as a case study)

- Made up of three independent studies tied together by a thematic narrative
- Each chapter can be thought of as a standalone research paper
- Work is often published separately (posters, conference papers, journal articles) before being compiled
- The thesis connects all studies through a coherent research proposition

---

## Conclusion

The core lesson is about **efficiency and accessibility** — both as a reader and as a writer of research:

- **As a reader**: follow the Abstract → Conclusion → Introduction → Full paper sequence to quickly assess relevance before investing significant time.
- **As a writer**: impose a clear structure with headings, figures, tables, and chapter summaries so that external researchers can navigate your work and extract value quickly.

Good research documents are not just about the content — they are about making that content discoverable and understandable to the widest possible audience.
