In this session, we'll deepen our understanding by looking at how cyber threats are classified, specifically, we'll describe threat origins, insider versus external, identify attack vectors, network versus endpoint, evaluate impact, financial versus reputational. By the end of this session, we'll be able to categorize threats based on who's behind them, how they operate, and what damage they cause. An insider threat comes from within the organization. It could be a current or former employee, contractor, or even a third-party partner with access to the internal systems. We generally divide insider threats into two types. Malicious insiders who act deliberately, perhaps out of revenge or personal gain. Negligent insiders who cause damage accidentally, for example, by losing a device or falling for a phishing scam. A real-world example is the 2020 Tesla incident where a staff member was offered money to install malware. Thankfully, they reported it, but it illustrates how damaging insiders can be. In contrast, external threats come from outside the organization. These include cybercriminals, hacker groups, state-sponsored actors, and hacktivists. A classic example is a ransomware gang that encrypts company files and demands payment in cryptocurrency. One high-profile case in the UK was the 2017 WannaCry attack on the National Health Service which disrupts services and affects thousands of appointments. That's an external threat with massive consequences. Now, let's move on to where threats occur. Network-based threats target organizations' digital infrastructure like servers, routers, or cloud systems. Here are a few examples. DDoS attacks, which overwhelm servers with traffic and knock services offline. Man-in-the-middle attacks, where an attacker secretly intercepts communication between two parties. Port scanning, used to identify vulnerable services that could be exploited. These attacks don't need access to a specific device. They exploit weaknesses in how systems talk to each other across the network. Endpoint threats target devices that people actually use, such as laptops, tablets, or smartphones. They are delivered via email, browser, or infected USBs. For instance, a phishing email tricks a user into revealing their password. A key logger records every keystroke to steal credentials. A ransomware file locks all the files on a user device and demands money to unlock them. These threats may seem small, but they can serve as an entry point for much larger attacks across a network. Let's now look at the impact, specifically financial damage caused by cyber threats. Financial impact might be immediate, like paying a ransom, or long term, like recovering from system downtime or paying legal fees. A good example is the Colonial Pipeline ransomware incident in 2021. The company paid nearly $3.5 million in ransom, and that's not including losses from disruption and repair. These attacks are not just technical issues. They are expensive business problems. Sometimes, the cost of a cyber attack isn't just financial, it's reputational. When sensitive data is exposed or systems are publicly compromised, people lose trust in the organization. One infamous case is the Sony Picture Hack in 2014, which leaked confidential emails and unreleased films. The result? Embarrassment. Legal fallout and years of brand damage. Reputational impact may not be immediate, but it can have lasting consequences, especially for a customer-facing organization. Here is a quick summary to tie everything together. When classifying threats, consider who is behind it. Is it an insider or an outsider? When does it occur? On the network or the device? What does it affect? The budget or the brand? Use this simple model to analyze threats and plan defense accordingly. Let's pause for a moment. If your organization was attacked, which would be more difficult to recover from?
