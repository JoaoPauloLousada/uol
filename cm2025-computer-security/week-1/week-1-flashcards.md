## Cluster 1 — Core Definitions (12 cards)

---

### Card 01
**Q:** What is a cyber threat?

**A:** Any event with the ability to strike a blow to missions, tasks, images, national cyber assets, or personnel through an information system — through unauthorized access, destruction, disclosure, alteration of information, or obstruction of service delivery.

*Source: [4-types-of-cyber-attacks-and-how-they-work/study-guide.md](4-types-of-cyber-attacks-and-how-they-work/study-guide.md) — Table 1*

---

### Card 02
**Q:** What is a cyber vulnerability?

**A:** A weakness within an asset, security procedures, or internal controls, or the implementation of a national cyber asset that can be exploited by internal or external threats.

*Source: [4-types-of-cyber-attacks-and-how-they-work/study-guide.md](4-types-of-cyber-attacks-and-how-they-work/study-guide.md) — Table 1*

---

### Card 03
**Q:** What is the NCSC definition of risk in cyber security terms?

**A:** Risk is the potential for a **threat** (a person or thing likely to cause damage) to exploit a **vulnerability** (a flaw, feature, or user error) that may result in some form of negative impact. Risk depends on: opportunities you present (your vulnerabilities), attacker capabilities, and attacker motivation.

*Source: [2-cyber-threat-landscape-the-most-common-attack/study-guide.md](2-cyber-threat-landscape-the-most-common-attack/study-guide.md) — Section 1.1*

---

### Card 04
**Q:** What is a cyber attack?

**A:** Any unauthorized cyber act aimed at violating the security policy of a cyber-asset and causing damage, disruption, or denial of services/access to information. Its purpose is to destroy and disrupt the operation of a computer network with political or security purposes.

*Source: [4-types-of-cyber-attacks-and-how-they-work/study-guide.md](4-types-of-cyber-attacks-and-how-they-work/study-guide.md) — Table 1 & Table 2*

---

### Card 05
**Q:** What distinguishes cyber-crime, cyber-attack, and cyber-warfare from each other?

**A:**
- **Cyber-crime:** Actions by non-governmental actors in violation of criminal law; the action itself is the violation regardless of outcome.
- **Cyber-attack:** Unauthorized act causing damage, disruption, or denial; result-oriented — qualifies as an attack if it leads to injury or financial damage.
- **Cyber-warfare:** The highest-level form of cyber-attack; carried out against national cyber interests with effects equivalent to an armed attack.

Cyber-attack sits at the intersection of cyber-crime and cyber-warfare.

*Source: [4-types-of-cyber-attacks-and-how-they-work/study-guide.md](4-types-of-cyber-attacks-and-how-they-work/study-guide.md) — Table 2 & Topic A*

---

### Card 06
**Q:** What is malware?

**A:** Malicious software — a broad category of programs designed to steal data, spy on activity, or destroy files. It acts like a virus for a computer.

*Source: [1-what-are-cyber-threats/subtitle.txt](1-what-are-cyber-threats/subtitle.txt)*

---

### Card 07
**Q:** What is phishing?

**A:** A bulk email attack that tricks users into revealing sensitive information (passwords, credit card details) or directs them to fake websites. One of the most common and successful cyber threats.

*Source: [1-what-are-cyber-threats/subtitle.txt](1-what-are-cyber-threats/subtitle.txt)*

---

### Card 08
**Q:** What is ransomware?

**A:** Malware that encrypts a victim's data and demands payment (typically cryptocurrency) to restore access. A growing and very costly threat; classified as an un-targeted attack technique.

*Source: [1-what-are-cyber-threats/subtitle.txt](1-what-are-cyber-threats/subtitle.txt) & [2-cyber-threat-landscape-the-most-common-attack/study-guide.md](2-cyber-threat-landscape-the-most-common-attack/study-guide.md) — Section 1.4*

---

### Card 09
**Q:** What is social engineering?

**A:** A psychological rather than technical attack. An attacker manipulates a target into revealing confidential information or granting access — for example, by impersonating tech support.

*Source: [1-what-are-cyber-threats/subtitle.txt](1-what-are-cyber-threats/subtitle.txt)*

---

### Card 10
**Q:** What is a supply chain attack?

**A:** An attack that targets less secure elements in an organisation's supply chain — for example, by infiltrating a third-party vendor or compromising software updates to eventually access a larger organisation. The SolarWinds attack is a prime example: attackers injected malware into a legitimate software update pushed to thousands of customers.

*Source: [1-what-are-cyber-threats/subtitle.txt](1-what-are-cyber-threats/subtitle.txt)*

---

### Card 11
**Q:** What is a cyber weapon?

**A:** A system designed and manufactured to change the structure or operation of other cyber systems. Examples: bot networks, logic bombs, cyber vulnerability exploitation software, malware, traffic generation systems.

*Source: [4-types-of-cyber-attacks-and-how-they-work/study-guide.md](4-types-of-cyber-attacks-and-how-they-work/study-guide.md) — Table 1*

---

### Card 12
**Q:** What is information manipulation and interference?

**A:** Spreading false information or altering legitimate data to cause confusion, influence decisions, or disrupt systems. This type of threat can impact elections, financial markets, and public trust.

*Source: [1-what-are-cyber-threats/subtitle.txt](1-what-are-cyber-threats/subtitle.txt)*

## Cluster 2 — Threat Classification (14 cards)

---

### Card 13
**Q:** What are the two types of insider threats?

**A:**
- **Malicious insiders:** Act deliberately, often motivated by revenge or personal gain.
- **Negligent insiders:** Cause damage accidentally — e.g., losing a device or falling for a phishing scam.

*Source: [3-classifying-cyber-threats/subtitle.txt](3-classifying-cyber-threats/subtitle.txt)*

---

### Card 14
**Q:** What distinguishes an insider threat from an external threat?

**A:** An **insider threat** comes from within the organisation — a current or former employee, contractor, or third-party partner with access to internal systems. An **external threat** comes from outside: cybercriminals, hacker groups, state-sponsored actors, or hacktivists.

*Source: [3-classifying-cyber-threats/subtitle.txt](3-classifying-cyber-threats/subtitle.txt)*

---

### Card 15
**Q:** What are the three examples of network-based attack vectors?

**A:**
- **DDoS attacks:** Overwhelm servers with traffic, knocking services offline.
- **Man-in-the-middle attacks:** Attacker secretly intercepts communication between two parties.
- **Port scanning:** Identifies vulnerable services that could be exploited.

Network attacks exploit weaknesses in how systems communicate — they do not require access to a specific device.

*Source: [3-classifying-cyber-threats/subtitle.txt](3-classifying-cyber-threats/subtitle.txt)*

---

### Card 16
**Q:** What are three examples of endpoint-based attack vectors?

**A:**
- **Phishing email:** Tricks a user into revealing their password.
- **Keylogger:** Records every keystroke to steal credentials.
- **Ransomware file:** Locks files on a user device and demands payment.

Endpoint threats target devices people actually use (laptops, tablets, smartphones) and can serve as entry points for larger network attacks.

*Source: [3-classifying-cyber-threats/subtitle.txt](3-classifying-cyber-threats/subtitle.txt)*

---

### Card 17
**Q:** What is financial impact in the context of cyber threats? Give a real-world example.

**A:** Financial impact includes immediate costs (ransom payments) and long-term costs (system downtime, legal fees, recovery). Example: the **2021 Colonial Pipeline** ransomware attack — the company paid nearly $3.5 million in ransom, not including disruption and repair losses.

*Source: [3-classifying-cyber-threats/subtitle.txt](3-classifying-cyber-threats/subtitle.txt)*

---

### Card 18
**Q:** What is reputational impact in the context of cyber threats? Give a real-world example.

**A:** Reputational impact occurs when sensitive data is exposed or systems are publicly compromised, causing loss of public trust and brand damage. Example: the **2014 Sony Pictures hack** leaked confidential emails and unreleased films, resulting in embarrassment, legal fallout, and years of brand damage. Reputational impact may not be immediate but can have lasting consequences.

*Source: [3-classifying-cyber-threats/subtitle.txt](3-classifying-cyber-threats/subtitle.txt)*

---

### Card 19
**Q:** What are the five attacker categories identified by the NCSC and their motivations?

**A:**
| Attacker | Motivation |
|---|---|
| Cyber criminals | Financial gain through fraud or sale of valuable information |
| Industrial competitors / foreign intelligence | Economic or diplomatic advantage |
| Hackers | Personal challenge and enjoyment |
| Hacktivists | Political or ideological motives |
| Employees / insiders | Personal gain, grievances, or accidental misuse |

*Source: [2-cyber-threat-landscape-the-most-common-attack/study-guide.md](2-cyber-threat-landscape-the-most-common-attack/study-guide.md) — Section 1.3*

---

### Card 20
**Q:** What is the key difference between un-targeted and targeted attacks?

**A:**
- **Un-targeted:** Attackers cast a wide net; victim identity is irrelevant; they exploit whoever is vulnerable.
- **Targeted:** Attacker has a specific interest in the organisation; attack is tailored to its systems, personnel, and processes; typically more damaging.

*Source: [2-cyber-threat-landscape-the-most-common-attack/study-guide.md](2-cyber-threat-landscape-the-most-common-attack/study-guide.md) — Section 1.4*

---

### Card 21
**Q:** What four techniques are used in un-targeted attacks?

**A:**
1. **Phishing** — bulk emails seeking sensitive information or directing to fake websites.
2. **Water holing** — compromising a legitimate website that target users regularly visit.
3. **Ransomware** — malware that encrypts data and demands payment.
4. **Scanning** — automated probing of the Internet for vulnerable systems.

*Source: [2-cyber-threat-landscape-the-most-common-attack/study-guide.md](2-cyber-threat-landscape-the-most-common-attack/study-guide.md) — Section 1.4*

---

### Card 22
**Q:** What three techniques are used in targeted attacks?

**A:**
1. **Spear-phishing** — tailored emails to specific individuals with malicious attachments or links.
2. **Deploying a botnet** — for DDoS against the specific organisation.
3. **Subverting the supply chain** — attacking equipment or software being delivered to the organisation.

*Source: [2-cyber-threat-landscape-the-most-common-attack/study-guide.md](2-cyber-threat-landscape-the-most-common-attack/study-guide.md) — Section 1.4*

---

### Card 23
**Q:** What is commodity capability? Give an example.

**A:** Off-the-shelf tools and techniques openly available on the Internet, requiring minimal skill and investment. Example: **Poison Ivy RAT** — a widely used Remote Access Tool. Commodity tools are effective precisely because organisations fail to apply basic security controls.

*Source: [2-cyber-threat-landscape-the-most-common-attack/study-guide.md](2-cyber-threat-landscape-the-most-common-attack/study-guide.md) — Section 1.2*

---

### Card 24
**Q:** What is bespoke capability? Give an example. How does it relate to commodity capability over time?

**A:** Tools and techniques developed for specific targets, requiring specialist knowledge. Example: **zero-day exploits**. Bespoke capabilities typically become commodity capabilities within days of their use being discovered — reusable exploits are quickly developed and shared. This means yesterday's targeted weapon becomes today's widely available toolkit.

*Source: [2-cyber-threat-landscape-the-most-common-attack/study-guide.md](2-cyber-threat-landscape-the-most-common-attack/study-guide.md) — Section 1.2 & Topic B*

---

### Card 25
**Q:** Why does the NCSC say every organisation is a potential victim?

**A:** Every organisation has something of value — information, systems, or infrastructure. If an organisation openly demonstrates weaknesses by failing to apply basic security controls, it will experience some form of cyber attack. Every organisation connected to the Internet should assume it will be the victim of an un-targeted attack.

*Source: [2-cyber-threat-landscape-the-most-common-attack/study-guide.md](2-cyber-threat-landscape-the-most-common-attack/study-guide.md) — Section 1.5*

---

### Card 26
**Q:** What are the six characteristics that make cyberspace uniquely difficult to defend?

**A:**
1. **Low cost of entry** — minimal investment needed to launch attacks.
2. **Anonymity** — attacker identity is difficult to confirm.
3. **Geographical uncertainty** — no fixed physical location.
4. **Lack of public transparency** — attacks occur invisibly.
5. **Scalability / wide range of effects** — unlike a bomb, cyber threats have very wide-ranging effects.
6. **Asymmetry** — a small number of individuals can effectively conduct or manage cyber warfare.

*Source: [4-types-of-cyber-attacks-and-how-they-work/study-guide.md](4-types-of-cyber-attacks-and-how-they-work/study-guide.md) — Section 2.2*

## Cluster 3 — Vulnerability Sources & Attack Model (14 cards)

---

### Card 27
**Q:** What are the three sources of vulnerabilities?

**A:**
1. **Flaws** — unintended functionality from poor design or implementation mistakes.
2. **Features** — intended functionality that can be misused.
3. **User error** — mistakes by administrators or users that undermine carefully designed systems.

Attackers may exploit any combination of these three sources.

*Source: [2-cyber-threat-landscape-the-most-common-attack/study-guide.md](2-cyber-threat-landscape-the-most-common-attack/study-guide.md) — Section 2.1*

---

### Card 28
**Q:** Give two examples of how features (intended functionality) have been misused in real attacks.

**A:**
1. **Microsoft Office macros:** Exploited by the Melissa worm (1999) and the Dridex banking Trojan (2014) to execute malicious code via Word documents.
2. **JavaScript:** Used by attackers to redirect browsers to malicious websites or silently download malware.

*Source: [2-cyber-threat-landscape-the-most-common-attack/study-guide.md](2-cyber-threat-landscape-the-most-common-attack/study-guide.md) — Section 2.1 & Q8*

---

### Card 29
**Q:** What is a zero-day vulnerability, why is it valuable, and what happens once it is discovered?

**A:** A zero-day is a recently discovered flaw that is **not yet publicly known** — vendors and anti-malware companies are unaware of it. It can fetch hundreds of thousands of pounds and is used primarily in bespoke attacks. Once publicly known, reusable exploits are rapidly developed, transitioning it from bespoke to **commodity capability** — posing risk to any system that has not yet patched.

*Source: [2-cyber-threat-landscape-the-most-common-attack/study-guide.md](2-cyber-threat-landscape-the-most-common-attack/study-guide.md) — Section 2.2*

---

### Card 30
**Q:** What are the four stages of the NCSC attack model? What framework is it derived from?

**A:** **Survey → Delivery → Breach → Affect**

Derived from the **Lockheed Martin Cyber Kill Chain**, simplified into four stages. The model applies regardless of whether an attack is targeted or un-targeted, and whether commodity or bespoke tools are used.

*Source: [2-cyber-threat-landscape-the-most-common-attack/study-guide.md](2-cyber-threat-landscape-the-most-common-attack/study-guide.md) — Section 3.1*

---

### Card 31
**Q:** What does an attacker look for during the Survey stage? Name four specific pieces of information.

**A:**
1. Open ports
2. Open services
3. Default settings that have not been changed
4. Vulnerable applications and operating systems (and network equipment makes/models)

Also: open-source intelligence (LinkedIn, Facebook, domain registration), technical forum posts by staff, and metadata in published documents (author, software version, file save location).

*Source: [2-cyber-threat-landscape-the-most-common-attack/study-guide.md](2-cyber-threat-landscape-the-most-common-attack/study-guide.md) — Section 3.2*

---

### Card 32
**Q:** What delivery methods does an attacker use to reach the target vulnerability?

**A:**
- Attempting to access online services directly
- Sending emails with malicious links or attachments
- Distributing infected USB sticks (e.g. at a trade fair)
- Creating fake websites for drive-by downloads
- For DDoS: making multiple connections to deny others access

*Source: [2-cyber-threat-landscape-the-most-common-attack/study-guide.md](2-cyber-threat-landscape-the-most-common-attack/study-guide.md) — Section 3.3*

---

### Card 33
**Q:** What does an attacker achieve at the Breach stage?

**A:** The attacker exploits a vulnerability (an unfixed flaw, misused feature, or user error) to:
- Make changes to the system's operation
- Gain access to online accounts
- Achieve full control of a computer, tablet, or smartphone

They can then impersonate the victim and move laterally to access other systems.

*Source: [2-cyber-threat-landscape-the-most-common-attack/study-guide.md](2-cyber-threat-landscape-the-most-common-attack/study-guide.md) — Section 3.4*

---

### Card 34
**Q:** What activities can an attacker carry out during the Affect stage?

**A:**
- **Retrieve information** — intellectual property or sensitive data
- **Make changes for financial benefit** — e.g. fraudulent payments
- **Disrupt operations** — e.g. DDoS, deleting operating systems
- **Consolidate and persist** — take over admin accounts, install scanning tools, disable monitoring
- **Exit cleanly** — remove all evidence
- **Create noise** — announce the breach publicly

*Source: [2-cyber-threat-landscape-the-most-common-attack/study-guide.md](2-cyber-threat-landscape-the-most-common-attack/study-guide.md) — Section 3.5*

---

### Card 35
**Q:** What is consolidation in the context of the Affect stage? Why is it the attacker's "Holy Grail"?

**A:** Consolidation is when the attacker establishes a persistent, expanding presence: taking over a user account, installing automated scanning tools, identifying additional systems, and disabling monitoring. Taking over an **administrator account** is the Holy Grail because it grants access to multiple systems — making eradication far harder once achieved.

*Source: [2-cyber-threat-landscape-the-most-common-attack/study-guide.md](2-cyber-threat-landscape-the-most-common-attack/study-guide.md) — Section 3.5 & Q11*

---

### Card 36
**Q:** What four stages does the Li & Liu paper describe for the anatomy of a cyber attack?

**A:**
1. **Target identification** — identifying the specific system, network, or individual to attack.
2. **Data collection** — gathering intelligence about vulnerabilities and defences.
3. **Perform a cyber-attack** — executing the attack.
4. **Investigation** — analysing results, establishing persistence, or covering tracks.

*Source: [4-types-of-cyber-attacks-and-how-they-work/study-guide.md](4-types-of-cyber-attacks-and-how-they-work/study-guide.md) — Section 1.2*

---

### Card 37
**Q:** What were the key facts of the WannaCry ransomware attack (2017)?

**A:** Infected over 200,000 computers in more than 150 countries. Hit UK NHS hospitals — surgeries were cancelled. Attackers demanded payment in Bitcoin to unlock files. Caused billions of dollars in damage.

*Source: [1-what-are-cyber-threats/subtitle.txt](1-what-are-cyber-threats/subtitle.txt)*

---

### Card 38
**Q:** What were the key facts of the Equifax data breach (2017)?

**A:** Hackers stole personal data — including Social Security numbers — of nearly 150 million people (almost half the US population). The breach occurred because of a vulnerability in unpatched software.

*Source: [1-what-are-cyber-threats/subtitle.txt](1-what-are-cyber-threats/subtitle.txt)*

---

### Card 39
**Q:** What were the key facts of the Uber data breach (2022)?

**A:** An 18-year-old affiliated with the Lapsus$ group purchased stolen employee credentials from the dark web, then launched an **MFA fatigue attack** — flooding an employee with approval requests until one was accepted. He then found hardcoded admin credentials in an internal network share, gaining access to AWS, G-Suite, Slack, and bug bounty reports. He announced the breach on the company's own Slack.

*Source: [week-1-summary.md](week-1-summary.md) — Section 8*

---

### Card 40
**Q:** What is an MFA fatigue attack?

**A:** An attacker repeatedly sends MFA push notification requests to a victim over a prolonged period, hoping the victim will eventually approve one out of frustration or confusion. It exploits the human element rather than a technical flaw in MFA itself.

*Source: [week-1-summary.md](week-1-summary.md) — Section 8*

## Cluster 4 — Controls & Defence (14 cards)

---

### Card 41
**Q:** What are the seven Cyber Essentials controls?

**A:**
1. Boundary firewalls and internet gateways
2. Malware protection
3. Patch management
4. Whitelisting and execution control
5. Secure configuration
6. Password policy
7. User access control

*Source: [2-cyber-threat-landscape-the-most-common-attack/study-guide.md](2-cyber-threat-landscape-the-most-common-attack/study-guide.md) — Section 4.2*

---

### Card 42
**Q:** What is the purpose of patch management, and why is it particularly critical at the Breach stage?

**A:** Patch management ensures patches are applied at the earliest opportunity, closing known software flaws before attackers can exploit them. All commodity malware depends on **known and predominantly patchable software flaws** — consistent patching is the single highest-impact control against commodity attacks. It was the missing control in all three NCSC case studies.

*Source: [2-cyber-threat-landscape-the-most-common-attack/study-guide.md](2-cyber-threat-landscape-the-most-common-attack/study-guide.md) — Section 4.2 & Q15*

---

### Card 43
**Q:** What is the purpose of whitelisting and execution control?

**A:** To prevent unknown software from running or installing. Includes disabling AutoRun on USB and CD drives. Stops malware and exploit tools from executing even if they are delivered to the system.

*Source: [2-cyber-threat-landscape-the-most-common-attack/study-guide.md](2-cyber-threat-landscape-the-most-common-attack/study-guide.md) — Section 4.2*

---

### Card 44
**Q:** What is secure configuration and what vulnerability source does it primarily address?

**A:** Restricting device, OS, and application functionality to the minimum needed for business operation. It primarily addresses **features** (intended functionality that can be misused) and limits the attack surface available to an attacker during the Delivery and Breach stages.

*Source: [2-cyber-threat-landscape-the-most-common-attack/study-guide.md](2-cyber-threat-landscape-the-most-common-attack/study-guide.md) — Section 4.2*

---

### Card 45
**Q:** What three additional controls does 10 Steps to Cyber Security add beyond Cyber Essentials?

**A:**
1. **Security monitoring** — identifies unexpected or suspicious activity.
2. **User training, education, and awareness** — staff understand their role and report unusual activity.
3. **Security incident management** — plans to deal with an attack and reduce business impact.

*Source: [2-cyber-threat-landscape-the-most-common-attack/study-guide.md](2-cyber-threat-landscape-the-most-common-attack/study-guide.md) — Section 4.3*

---

### Card 46
**Q:** What is defence-in-depth and why does the NCSC recommend it?

**A:** A strategy of establishing multiple layered security mechanisms so that an attacker who bypasses one layer still faces additional barriers. Recommended because even motivated attackers typically start with commodity tools — multiple consistent controls mean failure of any single control does not result in a successful attack.

*Source: [2-cyber-threat-landscape-the-most-common-attack/study-guide.md](2-cyber-threat-landscape-the-most-common-attack/study-guide.md) — Section 4.1 & Q16*

---

### Card 47
**Q:** Which security controls primarily mitigate the Survey stage?

**A:**
- **User education and awareness** — staff understand what to publish online and how to handle metadata.
- **Secure configuration** — limits information disclosed by internet-facing systems (e.g. removing default system settings that broadcast network information).

*Source: [2-cyber-threat-landscape-the-most-common-attack/study-guide.md](2-cyber-threat-landscape-the-most-common-attack/study-guide.md) — Section 4.4*

---

### Card 48
**Q:** Which security controls primarily mitigate the Delivery stage?

**A:**
- Network perimeter defences (firewalls, web proxy, web filtering)
- Malware protection (blocks malicious emails and downloads)
- Password policy (prevents weak credentials; locks accounts after failed attempts)
- Secure configuration (reduces the available attack surface)

*Source: [2-cyber-threat-landscape-the-most-common-attack/study-guide.md](2-cyber-threat-landscape-the-most-common-attack/study-guide.md) — Section 4.4*

---

### Card 49
**Q:** What happened in NCSC Case Study 1 (UK Energy Sector espionage campaign) and what control stopped it?

**A:** Attackers compromised websites of multiple energy companies managed by a single web design company, inserting scripts that redirected visitors' browsers to download a RAT via known Java and browser vulnerabilities. The RAT began sending beacons to attacker-controlled domains. **Security monitoring** detected the command-and-control traffic before the Affect stage could complete.

*Source: [2-cyber-threat-landscape-the-most-common-attack/study-guide.md](2-cyber-threat-landscape-the-most-common-attack/study-guide.md) — Section 5.1*

---

### Card 50
**Q:** What happened in NCSC Case Study 2 (300 computers infected) and what lesson does it teach about supply chains?

**A:** Attackers found that a UK company's corporate website was hosted by a service provider with a known vulnerability. They injected a script targeting visitors from the company's IP range, infecting **300+ computers** with a RAT via a known software flaw. Detected early by network security monitoring.

**Lesson:** An organisation's security is only as strong as its suppliers'. Third-party contracts must require equivalent security standards.

*Source: [2-cyber-threat-landscape-the-most-common-attack/study-guide.md](2-cyber-threat-landscape-the-most-common-attack/study-guide.md) — Section 5.2*

---

### Card 51
**Q:** What happened in NCSC Case Study 3 (spear-phishing on a system administrator) and why was the admin a high-value target?

**A:** Attackers identified a system administrator's personal interests via open-source intelligence, then sent a socially-engineered email to their personal address with a Trojanised document. The admin was repeatedly prompted to run an executable until they clicked OK. A RAT established persistence and exfiltrated screenshots for nearly a week before monitoring detected the transfers.

**Why high-value:** Admins have privileged access to multiple systems — the "Holy Grail" for an attacker. Least privilege must apply even to admins' day-to-day browsing.

*Source: [2-cyber-threat-landscape-the-most-common-attack/study-guide.md](2-cyber-threat-landscape-the-most-common-attack/study-guide.md) — Section 5.3*

---

### Card 52
**Q:** Across all three NCSC case studies, which two controls appear most consistently as mitigations and what does this tell us?

**A:** **Patching** and **network perimeter defences** appear in all three. This shows that the majority of attacks — including targeted, multi-stage attacks — exploit **known and patchable vulnerabilities** delivered via **network-accessible pathways**. The challenge is not knowing what to do — it is **consistently doing it** across the organisation and its suppliers.

*Source: [2-cyber-threat-landscape-the-most-common-attack/study-guide.md](2-cyber-threat-landscape-the-most-common-attack/study-guide.md) — Q20*

---

### Card 53
**Q:** What mitigations would have prevented the Uber 2022 breach?

**A:**
- **Phishing-resistant MFA** (e.g. hardware security keys or number-matching) — defeats MFA fatigue attacks.
- **Credential management tooling** — eliminates hardcoded credentials in network shares.
- **Phishing awareness training** — reduces likelihood of employees approving unsolicited MFA requests.

*Source: [week-1-summary.md](week-1-summary.md) — Section 8*

---

### Card 54
**Q:** What is the key strategic principle for breaking an attack pattern?

**A:** Even highly motivated attackers typically start with commodity tools because they are cheaper and easier. Security controls that disrupt **any stage** of the Survey → Delivery → Breach → Affect model can prevent the attack from succeeding. Implementing Cyber Essentials-level controls is the highest-value defensive investment because it defeats the vast majority of attacks.

*Source: [2-cyber-threat-landscape-the-most-common-attack/study-guide.md](2-cyber-threat-landscape-the-most-common-attack/study-guide.md) — Section 4.1*

## Cluster 5 — CIA Triad, Attack Types & CTI (14 cards)

### Card 55
**Q:** What are the three principles of the CIA triad?

**A:**
- **Confidentiality:** Only authorized sources can access sensitive information and functions (e.g. military secrets).
- **Integrity:** Only authorized individuals and resources can modify, add, or remove sensitive information and functions.
- **Availability:** Systems, functions, and data must be available on demand based on agreed SLA service levels.

### Card 56
**Q:** What is the difference between a virus and a worm?

**A:** A **virus** is a self-replicating program that spreads by inserting a copy of itself into files; it **requires human intervention** to spread (e.g. a user opens an infected file). A **worm** is an autonomous program that **self-propagates** across a network without human intervention.

*Source: [4-types-of-cyber-attacks-and-how-they-work/study-guide.md](4-types-of-cyber-attacks-and-how-they-work/study-guide.md) — Section 3.1 & Q3*

### Card 57
**Q:** What is the difference between a DoS and a DDoS attack? Which CIA principle does each primarily target?

**A:**
- **DoS (Denial of Service):** A single attacker floods the target with messages, blocking legitimate data flow and consuming all server resources.
- **DDoS (Distributed DoS):** Same goal but launched simultaneously from many distributed systems (often a botnet), making it much harder to block.

Both primarily target **Availability**.

*Source: [4-types-of-cyber-attacks-and-how-they-work/study-guide.md](4-types-of-cyber-attacks-and-how-they-work/study-guide.md) — Section 3.1 & Topic B*

### Card 58
**Q:** What is a logic bomb and which CIA principle does it primarily target?

**A:** A logic bomb is malicious code entered into a program by a programmer (often an insider) that **automatically performs a destructive activity when a specific event occurs** (e.g. a date, login, or file deletion). It primarily targets **Integrity** — lying dormant within legitimate code until triggered.

*Source: [4-types-of-cyber-attacks-and-how-they-work/study-guide.md](4-types-of-cyber-attacks-and-how-they-work/study-guide.md) — Section 3.1 & Topic B*

### Card 59
**Q:** What is a Trojan horse and how does it differ from a virus?

**A:** A Trojan horse **hides dangerous code inside a program that appears helpful** — the user willingly runs it. It does not self-replicate. A virus, by contrast, self-replicates by inserting copies into other files and spreads independently of user intent (though it requires human intervention to initially spread).

*Source: [4-types-of-cyber-attacks-and-how-they-work/study-guide.md](4-types-of-cyber-attacks-and-how-they-work/study-guide.md) — Section 3.1 & Q10*

### Card 60
**Q:** What is a botnet and why is it particularly dangerous?

**A:** A network of infected systems under remote attacker control, used to distribute malware, coordinate attacks, send spam, and steal data. Also called **electronic soldiers**. Particularly dangerous because: (1) one operator can scale across thousands of machines; (2) they are the platform for DDoS attacks; (3) they simultaneously threaten all three CIA pillars — Confidentiality, Integrity, and Availability.

*Source: [4-types-of-cyber-attacks-and-how-they-work/study-guide.md](4-types-of-cyber-attacks-and-how-they-work/study-guide.md) — Section 3.1 & Q9*

### Card 61
**Q:** What is a sniffer and which CIA principle does it target?

**A:** A sniffer eavesdrops on routed network information, examining each packet in the data stream to extract usernames and passwords. It primarily targets **Confidentiality**.

*Source: [4-types-of-cyber-attacks-and-how-they-work/study-guide.md](4-types-of-cyber-attacks-and-how-they-work/study-guide.md) — Section 3.1*

### Card 62
**Q:** What are the seven types of cyber security and what does each protect?

**A:**
| Type | Protects |
|---|---|
| Network security | Computer networks from malware and hacking |
| Infrastructure security | Critical infrastructure components |
| Application security | Applications using anti-virus, encryption, firewalls |
| Information security | Physical and digital data from unauthorized access, disclosure, or modification |
| Operational security | Data handling processes; defines user permissions |
| Cloud security | Information stored in the cloud |
| End-user training | The human element — teaches users to avoid risky behaviour |

*Source: [4-types-of-cyber-attacks-and-how-they-work/study-guide.md](4-types-of-cyber-attacks-and-how-they-work/study-guide.md) — Section 4.2*

### Card 63
**Q:** What is the key difference between a national and a corporate cyber-security policy?

**A:** **National policy** covers all citizens and foreign businesspeople within its jurisdiction — broad and comprehensive. **Corporate policy** applies only to employees and those with a formal legal contract — resource providers and supply chain partners are not automatically bound. This creates a structural vulnerability that policy alone cannot close without explicit contractual requirements.

*Source: [4-types-of-cyber-attacks-and-how-they-work/study-guide.md](4-types-of-cyber-attacks-and-how-they-work/study-guide.md) — Section 5.2*

### Card 64
**Q:** What are the four types of Cyber Threat Intelligence (CTI) and what does each cover?

**A:**
| Type | What it covers |
|---|---|
| **Strategic** | Attacker motivation, historical patterns, long-term trends |
| **Tactical** | Attack techniques and TTPs (Tactics, Techniques, Procedures) |
| **Operational** | Real-time or near-real-time activity, timelines, active infrastructure |
| **Technical** | Machine-readable artefacts: IOCs — domains, IPs, file hashes, malware names |

### Card 65
**Q:** What three questions does a cyber threat analysis answer?

**A:**
1. **What is happening?** — threat characterisation
2. **How credible and relevant is the intelligence?** — source assessment
3. **What should be done about it?** — mitigation

### Card 66
**Q:** What are the five intelligence source types in CTI assessment and their typical reliability levels?

**A:**
| Source | Reliability |
|---|---|
| Internal logs / SIEM data | High — first-party, directly observed |
| Government advisories (NCSC, CISA) | High — validated, authoritative |
| Sector OSINT / peer sharing | Medium — useful but needs correlation |
| Commercial threat feeds | Medium — timely but may have false positives |
| Dark web forums | Low–Medium — unverified, adversarial environment |

### Card 67
**Q:** What are the five key signal words that identify each CTI type when reading a threat report?

**A:**
- **Strategic:** "motivation", "historically", "geopolitical", "financially motivated", "long-term campaign"
- **Tactical:** "phishing", "lateral movement", "credential theft", "TTPs", "attack pattern"
- **Operational:** "current campaign", "timeline", "active infrastructure", "observed on [date]"
- **Technical:** file hashes, IP addresses, domain names, malware names, CVE numbers, registry keys

### Card 68
**Q:** What are the most common mistakes to avoid in a cyber threat analysis?

**A:**
| Mistake | Fix |
|---|---|
| Summarising the report without analysing it | Add your own inference — what does it mean? what does it imply? |
| Generic mitigations with no link to the threat | Justify each mitigation by referencing the specific attack step it counters |
| Listing IOCs without assessing their value | State what the IOC tells you and how it should be operationalised |
| Ignoring source reliability | Assess every cited source for confidence level |
| Overstating certainty | Use hedged language: "attributed to", "suspected", "with high confidence" |
