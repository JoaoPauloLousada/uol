Case Study: The Uber Data Breach (2022)
=======================================

1. Summary
----------
In September 2022, an 18-year-old hacker affiliated with the cybercriminal group
Lapsus$ gained unauthorised access to Uber's internal systems. The attacker began
by purchasing stolen employee credentials from a dark web marketplace. Because the
account was protected by multi-factor authentication (MFA), credential theft alone
was not enough. The hacker then contacted the employee directly via WhatsApp,
impersonating an Uber IT staff member, while simultaneously flooding the employee's
phone with MFA approval requests for over an hour — a technique known as MFA
fatigue. Eventually the employee approved one of the prompts, granting the attacker
full network access. Once inside, the hacker discovered hardcoded admin credentials
in an internal network share, which provided elevated access to Uber's AWS
environment, G-Suite, Slack, internal dashboards, and bug bounty reports. The
attacker then announced the breach directly on Uber's Slack.

2. Classification
-----------------
- Source: External threat — the attacker had no prior authorised access to Uber's
  systems and acted as part of an external criminal group (Lapsus$).

- Vector: Endpoint and network — the initial compromise targeted a single employee's
  device/account (endpoint) via social engineering. Once inside, the attacker moved
  laterally across the corporate network, accessing cloud services and internal tools.

- Impact:
  - Operational: Uber temporarily shut down Slack, internal tools, and codebases to
    contain the breach, disrupting day-to-day operations.
  - Reputational: The public announcement via the company's own Slack caused
    significant embarrassment and renewed scrutiny of Uber's security posture.
  - Financial: While no direct ransom was paid, Uber faced regulatory scrutiny and
    remediation costs, including credential rotation and system audits.

3. Response and Outcome
-----------------------
Uber's incident response included disabling compromised accounts, rotating
credentials and access keys, locking down the codebase, and temporarily taking
internal tools offline. The attacker was later identified and arrested in the UK.
Uber confirmed that no user payment data or personal trip history was accessed,
though internal communications and vulnerability reports were exposed. The breach
prompted Uber to overhaul MFA policies and tighten access controls.

4. Critical Reflection
----------------------
The core failure was not technical but human: a well-protected account was bypassed
by exploiting employee trust and fatigue. Key mistakes included the absence of
MFA fatigue-resistant authentication (such as hardware security keys or number
matching), hardcoded credentials left exposed in an internal file share, and
insufficient employee training to recognise social engineering over messaging apps.
This case illustrates that layered defences — strong credentials and MFA — are
undermined if the human element is not equally hardened. It also shows why accurate
threat classification matters: recognising this as an endpoint/social-engineering
vector, rather than a purely technical intrusion, would have directed defences
toward user awareness and phishing-resistant MFA from the outset.
