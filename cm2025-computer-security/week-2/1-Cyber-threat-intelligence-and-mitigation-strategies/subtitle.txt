Welcome back. In today's session, we'll be exploring Cyber Threat Intelligence, often referred to as a CTI, and how it plays a central role in developing effective mitigation strategy. To learn how CTI works, the different forms it takes, and how we apply this intelligence to strength cyber defenses, let's begin by defining what CTI actually is. What is Cyber Threat Intelligence? CTI is the process of collecting and analyzing data to understand potential or active threats targeting your systems, rather than just reacting to incidents. CTI provides contextual, timely information that can help organizations predict attacks and prepare effective defenses. It includes information on who the attacker is, why they are attacking, how the executor attacks, what indicators you can look for to detect them early. There are four main types of CTI, each serving different audiences and purposes. Strategic Intelligence is at the highest level and is aimed at senior leadership. It covers broad trends and emerging risks, helping with long-term planning. Next is Tactical Intelligence, which focuses on how attackers operate, their tools, techniques, and procedures. This is valuable to security analysts. Operational Intelligence at the next level provides real-time insight into ongoing campaigns often used to adjust defenses immediately. Technical Intelligence includes specific details such as malware hashes, malicious IP addresses, or phishing domains. It's the most granular form and directly useful for blocking threats. Why CTI matters? Cyber Threat Intelligence enhances organization decision-making by transforming raw data into actionable knowledge. It allows organizations to detect attacks earlier, assess risks more accurately, and respond with greater precision. Instead of relying solely on generic defenses, organizations can apply intelligence to tailor their security control based on who is targeting them and how. What are Intelligent Sources? CTI can be gathered from both internal and external sources. Internally, organizations can generate valuable intelligence through system logs, incident reports, and user activity. Externally, organizations might rely on government advisories, such as the National Cyber Security Center, NCCS in the UK, and the Cyber Security and Infrastructure Cyber Agency, CISA, in the US. Open-source platforms and forums known as OSINT, sector-specific communities, called ISACS, where peers share threat data. Commercial intelligence feeds, provided by cybersecurity vendors, and logs. The most effective CTI strategies combine multiple sources for a fuller picture. Turning intelligence into action. Collecting intelligence is the first step. The real value comes when that intelligence is applied effectively. This process involves collecting data from various trusted sources, analyzing the relevance and accuracy of that data, correlating it with the organization's own environment, acting by blocking malicious domain, updating firewall rules, or issuing alerts. And finally, reviewing and refining defenses. This is a continuous cycle. The threat landscape changes daily. Let's now look at mitigation strategies. There are practical defenses informed by threat intelligence, and they present layers of defense. Here are examples from each layer, starting with the outermost layer and finishing with the innermost layer. Enforcing multi-factor authentication, MFA, to block unauthorized accesses. Using endpoint protection and firewall to reduce attack surfaces. Conducting self-awareness training to prevent social engineering attacks. Performing regular patching and updates to fix vulnerabilities. Maintaining secure backups, which are tested, and enabling continuous network monitoring and alerts for suspicious activity. No single measure is enough. It's about building layers of defense. Case example, ransomware defense. Take ransomware as a common threat. You can't rely on just one solution. You need a coordinated approach. Here, how to defend effectively. Block phishing emails before users see them with filters and training. Apply the principle of least privilege, so attackers cannot move freely by limiting user access. Ensure off-site backups are done regularly. Develop an incident response plan that's been tested and use monitoring tools to detect lateral movement. Each one adds resilience and reduces the risk of full compromise. To summarize, CTI is critical to staying ahead of evolving threats. It's not just about gathering data. It's about making sense of it, acting on it, and building smarter defenses. Mitigation is most effective when it's layered, informed by CTI, and continually refined. Stay updated, stay alert, and always plan for the unexpected.
